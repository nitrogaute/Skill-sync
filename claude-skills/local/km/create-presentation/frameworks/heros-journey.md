# Hero's Journey (Simplified for Presentations)

## When to Use

Best for keynote speeches, inspirational talks, customer success stories, and founder narratives. Use when you want the audience to emotionally connect with a transformation story — the hero (a person, team, company, or the audience themselves) faces a challenge, struggles through it, and emerges transformed.

## Structure

### 1. The Ordinary World (1–2 slides)
Set the scene — who is the hero, what was life like before the challenge? This grounds the audience in a relatable starting point.

**Purpose**: Create identification. The audience should see themselves or someone they know.

### 2. The Challenge (2–3 slides)
Something disrupts the ordinary world — a problem, opportunity, or catalyst that forces the hero to act. Show the stakes and why ignoring it wasn't an option.

**Purpose**: Create tension and investment in the outcome.

### 3. The Struggle (2–3 slides)
The hero encounters obstacles, makes mistakes, learns, and adapts. This is where the story gets real — don't skip the hard parts. Vulnerability builds credibility and connection.

**Purpose**: Show authenticity. Audiences distrust stories without difficulty.

### 4. The Transformation (2–3 slides)
The hero applies what they've learned and achieves the breakthrough. Show the turning point and the new capability, insight, or result that emerged.

**Purpose**: Deliver the payoff. This is the emotional peak.

### 5. The New World (1–2 slides)
What's different now? What was gained? And crucially — what does this mean for the audience? Connect the hero's transformation to the audience's own situation.

**Purpose**: Make the story relevant and actionable for the people in the room.

## Slide Skeleton

| Slide | Section | Purpose | Key Question It Answers |
|-------|---------|---------|------------------------|
| 1 | Title | Set expectations | What story are we telling? |
| 2 | Ordinary World | The starting point | Who is the hero? Where did they start? |
| 3 | Challenge | The catalyst | What changed? |
| 4 | Challenge | The stakes | What was at risk? |
| 5 | Struggle | The obstacles | What went wrong? |
| 6 | Struggle | The learning | What did they discover? |
| 7 | Transformation | The breakthrough | What was the turning point? |
| 8 | Transformation | The result | What was achieved? |
| 9 | New World | The new reality | What's different now? |
| 10 | New World | The lesson for us | What does this mean for you? |
| 11 | Call to action | The invitation | What should we do with this? |
| 12 | Q&A | Discussion | Open floor |

## Example Transitions

- Ordinary World → Challenge: "Then everything changed..."
- Challenge → Struggle: "It wasn't easy. In fact, the first thing we tried failed completely..."
- Struggle → Transformation: "That's when we realized something we'd been missing all along..."
- Transformation → New World: "Today, looking back, the difference is remarkable..."
- New World → Audience: "And here's why this matters for every one of you..."

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Skipping the struggle**: A story without difficulty is just bragging. The struggle is what makes the transformation meaningful and credible.
- **Making it about yourself when it shouldn't be**: If the hero should be the customer, the team, or the audience — don't make it a founder ego trip. The best presenters make the audience the hero.
- **No connection to the audience**: A great story that doesn't connect to the audience's situation is just entertainment. Always close with "what this means for you."
- **Too much detail in the Ordinary World**: Get to the challenge fast. 1–2 slides of setup is enough. The audience is waiting for the tension.
- **Manufactured emotion**: Don't force dramatic language onto undramatic content. If it's a product launch, not a life-or-death story, calibrate the emotional intensity accordingly.
