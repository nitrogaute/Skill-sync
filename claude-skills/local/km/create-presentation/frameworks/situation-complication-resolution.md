# Situation → Complication → Resolution (SCR / Minto Pyramid)

## When to Use

Best for executive and board-level presentations, business cases, investment proposals, and strategic recommendations. Use when your audience expects a structured logical argument and needs to make a decision.

## Structure

### 1. Situation (2–3 slides)
Establish the current state — what's true today that everyone can agree on. This is neutral ground. No opinions yet, just facts and context the audience already knows or can easily verify.

**Purpose**: Build shared understanding and credibility. Signal that you've done your homework.

### 2. Complication (3–4 slides)
Introduce the tension — what has changed, what's at risk, what's not working, or what opportunity is being missed. This is where you create the "so what?" urgency that makes the resolution feel necessary rather than optional.

**Purpose**: Create intellectual and emotional pressure for action. Make the status quo feel untenable.

### 3. Resolution (remaining slides)
Present your recommended path forward — the solution, strategy, or decision you're asking for. Back it with evidence, timeline, risk mitigation, and clear next steps.

**Purpose**: Provide a credible, actionable answer to the tension you created.

## Slide Skeleton

| Slide | Section | Purpose | Key Question It Answers |
|-------|---------|---------|------------------------|
| 1 | Title | Set expectations | What is this about? |
| 2 | Agenda | Roadmap | What will we cover? |
| 3 | Situation | Market/industry context | Where are we today? |
| 4 | Situation | Our current position | How are we positioned? |
| 5 | Complication | The problem/change | What has changed? |
| 6 | Complication | Impact if we don't act | What's at stake? |
| 7 | Complication | Why now? | Why is this urgent? |
| 8 | Resolution | Proposed approach | What should we do? |
| 9 | Resolution | Evidence/data | Why will this work? |
| 10 | Resolution | Timeline & milestones | When and how? |
| 11 | Resolution | Risk mitigation | What could go wrong? |
| 12 | Resolution | Ask/next steps | What do we need from you? |
| 13 | Summary | Key takeaway | What's the one thing to remember? |
| 14 | Q&A | Discussion | Open floor |

## Example Transitions

- Situation → Complication: "That's where we stand today. But there's a challenge we need to address..."
- Complication → Resolution: "Given these pressures, here's our recommended path forward..."
- Resolution → Ask: "To make this happen, we need the following decisions today..."
- Any section → Summary: "Let me bring it all together with the key takeaway..."

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Spending too long on Situation**: Your audience already knows the basics. 2–3 slides max. If you're past slide 4 and haven't reached the Complication, you're losing them.
- **Weak Complication**: If the Complication doesn't create genuine tension, the Resolution feels unnecessary. "Things are fine but could be better" is not a Complication.
- **Resolution without evidence**: The Resolution needs data, not just assertions. "We should do X" is weak. "We should do X because of Y, Z, and here's proof" is strong.
- **Burying the ask**: Put your ask/decision request on a dedicated slide, not as a bullet on the summary. Make it impossible to miss.
- **Conflating Situation and Complication**: The Situation is neutral; the Complication is where tension lives. Keep them separate.
