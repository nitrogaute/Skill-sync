# STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result)

## When to Use

Best for case studies, project retrospectives, post-mortems, lessons-learned presentations, and any narrative that tells the story of a specific initiative from start to finish. Use when the audience needs to understand what was done, why, and what came of it — especially for replicable processes or investment justification.

## Structure

### 1. Situation (1–2 slides)
Set the context — what was the environment, the constraints, the starting conditions? Give the audience enough background to understand why the task mattered and what made it challenging.

**Purpose**: Ground the audience in the real-world conditions. No idealized setup — show the messiness.

### 2. Task (1–2 slides)
Define the specific objective — what were you trying to achieve? What were the success criteria, the constraints, and the timeline? Be precise about scope.

**Purpose**: Clarify the goal so the audience can evaluate whether the actions and results were appropriate.

### 3. Action (3–4 slides)
Describe what was actually done — the approach, the key decisions, the methodology, the tools, the team. This is the meat of the case study. Be specific about what worked, what was tried and failed, and why you made the choices you did.

**Purpose**: Show the work. This is where the audience learns something replicable or evaluates your judgment.

### 4. Result (2–3 slides)
Present the outcomes — quantified where possible. What was achieved vs. the original task? What were the side effects, unexpected learnings, and lasting changes? Connect back to the original situation to show the delta.

**Purpose**: Prove impact and extract lessons. Close the loop.

## Slide Skeleton

| Slide | Section | Purpose | Key Question It Answers |
|-------|---------|---------|------------------------|
| 1 | Title | Set expectations | What case are we presenting? |
| 2 | Situation | Context and constraints | What was the environment? |
| 3 | Situation | Why this mattered | Why was this worth doing? |
| 4 | Task | The objective | What were we trying to achieve? |
| 5 | Task | Success criteria | How would we measure success? |
| 6 | Action | Approach overview | What did we do? |
| 7 | Action | Key decision #1 | Why did we choose this path? |
| 8 | Action | Key decision #2 | What was the hardest choice? |
| 9 | Action | Execution details | How did we implement? |
| 10 | Result | Quantified outcomes | What did we achieve? |
| 11 | Result | Lessons learned | What would we do differently? |
| 12 | Result | Applicability | How can others apply this? |
| 13 | Summary | Key takeaway | What's the one thing? |
| 14 | Q&A | Discussion | Open floor |

## Example Transitions

- Situation → Task: "Given these conditions, our objective was clear..."
- Task → Action: "Here's how we went about it..."
- Action → Result: "So, did it work? Let me show you the numbers..."
- Result → Lessons: "Beyond the metrics, here's what we learned that we didn't expect..."
- Result → Applicability: "And here's how this applies to what you're working on..."

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Glossing over failures in Action**: The most valuable part of a case study is often what didn't work and why. Audiences learn more from honest accounts of difficulty than from polished success narratives.
- **Vague results**: "It went well" is not a result. "Reduced cycle time by 34% and saved €2.1M annually" is. Quantify everything you can.
- **Disconnected Task and Result**: If your Result doesn't directly address the success criteria you defined in Task, the audience will notice the gap. Close the loop explicitly.
- **Too much Action detail**: Technical audiences love the Action section, but don't let it become a blow-by-blow. Focus on the 3–4 decisions that mattered most, not every step.
- **No "so what" for the audience**: Always connect the case back to the audience's context. "Here's how this applies to your situation" is the line that makes a case study useful rather than just interesting.
