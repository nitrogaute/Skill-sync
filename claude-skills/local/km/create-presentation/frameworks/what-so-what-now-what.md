# What → So What → Now What

## When to Use

Best for data-driven updates, progress reports, quarterly reviews, research findings, and status presentations. Use when you have facts/data and need to help the audience understand their implications and decide what to do about them. Particularly effective for technical audiences who want evidence before recommendations.

Also excellent for **change initiatives** and **new process introductions** where you need to:
- Explain what's changing (WHAT)
- Show how it connects to the bigger picture (SO WHAT)  
- Give concrete next steps (NOW WHAT)

## Structure

This framework is **flexible in slide count**. The three-section structure is mandatory; the number of slides per section scales with content complexity.

### Compressed Format (3–5 slides)
For short all-hands updates, 10-minute briefings, or tight executive slots:
- **1 slide per section** — one WHAT, one SO WHAT, one NOW WHAT
- Each slide carries the full narrative for that section
- Speaker notes do the heavy lifting; slides show only anchors

### Standard Format (8–12 slides)
For detailed presentations, workshops, or training sessions:

### 1. What (3–4 slides)
Present the facts, data, findings, or observations. Be objective and clear. Use charts, tables, and concrete numbers. No spin, no recommendations yet — just what happened or what you found.

**Purpose**: Establish the factual foundation. Build trust through transparency and rigor.

### 2. So What (2–3 slides)
Interpret the data — what do these facts mean? What are the implications, trends, risks, or opportunities? This is your analysis layer. Connect dots that the audience might not connect on their own.

**Purpose**: Add insight to information. Move from "here's the data" to "here's why it matters."

### 3. Now What (2–3 slides)
Based on the implications, what should we do? Present recommendations, options, or decisions that need to be made. Be specific about actions, owners, and timelines.

**Purpose**: Convert insight into action. Don't leave the audience with analysis paralysis.

## Slide Skeleton

| Slide | Section | Purpose | Key Question It Answers |
|-------|---------|---------|------------------------|
| 1 | Title | Set expectations | What is this update about? |
| 2 | Agenda | Roadmap | What will we cover? |
| 3 | What | Key metrics/data overview | What happened? |
| 4 | What | Detailed findings #1 | What does the data show? |
| 5 | What | Detailed findings #2 | What else did we find? |
| 6 | What | Trend analysis | Where is this heading? |
| 7 | So What | Interpretation | What does this mean? |
| 8 | So What | Risks and opportunities | What's at stake? |
| 9 | So What | Comparison/benchmark | How does this compare? |
| 10 | Now What | Recommendations | What should we do? |
| 11 | Now What | Timeline and owners | Who does what, when? |
| 12 | Now What | Decision needed | What do we need to decide today? |
| 13 | Summary | Key takeaway | What's the one thing? |
| 14 | Q&A | Discussion | Open floor |

## Example Transitions

- What → So What: "So what does all this data actually tell us?"
- So What → Now What: "Given these implications, here's what I recommend we do..."
- Within What: "Let me drill into the numbers behind this trend..."
- Now What → Summary: "To summarize: the data shows X, which means Y, so we should do Z."
- **For virtual presentations**: "So, let's see this in context — next slide please."
- **Handoff to next speaker**: "I'll hand it over to [Name]."

## Change Management Variant

When introducing a new process, initiative, or way of working, adapt the framework:

### WHAT Section — The Initiative
- Open with a **relatable pain point** the audience has experienced (not data)
- Explain the name deliberately if it has meaning
- State what's being introduced clearly
- **Immediately clarify what's NOT changing** — "This is not a new process, it's simply the existing process sharpened"
- End with the value chain: Better X → Better Y → Stronger Z → Investment in people

### SO WHAT Section — The System
- Show visually how the pieces connect (infographic, system diagram)
- Walk through the flow from bottom-up (foundation → aggregation → outcomes)
- Explain each KPI, metric, or component with a one-liner
- Acknowledge data quality requirements: "The dashboard is only as good as the data feeding it"
- Show both sides: "Why this matters" vs. "What we want to avoid"

### NOW WHAT Section — The Ask
- Start with "what this means in practice"
- Separate "what stays the same" from "what's new"
- Be specific about the ask: "What we need from you is X by [date]"
- Acknowledge difficulty and offer support: "We know this can be challenging... if you need help, contact me or [Name]"
- Clear handoff if another speaker follows

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Data dump without interpretation**: Showing 15 charts without explaining what they mean is not a presentation — it's a spreadsheet tour. The "So What" is where your value as a presenter lives.
- **Jumping straight to recommendations**: If you skip the data, your recommendations feel unsupported. If you skip the "So What", they feel unconnected. Earn the right to recommend.
- **Cherry-picking data**: Audiences (especially technical ones) will notice if you only present data that supports your conclusion. Show the full picture, including uncomfortable data, and address it honestly.
- **Vague "Now What"**: "We should monitor this" is not actionable. Specify what gets monitored, by whom, how often, and what triggers escalation.
- **Too many slides in "What"**: If your data section is 10 slides and your recommendations are 1, the balance is off. Consolidate data, expand implications and actions.
