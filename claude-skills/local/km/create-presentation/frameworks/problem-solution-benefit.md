# Problem → Solution → Benefit

## When to Use

Best for product pitches, sales presentations, proposals, and any situation where you're asking someone to buy, adopt, or invest in something specific. Use when your audience has a pain point you can solve and needs to see clear value.

## Structure

### 1. Problem (2–3 slides)
Define the pain point in the audience's own language. Make them feel understood. Use their metrics, their frustrations, their constraints. This is not your problem — it's theirs.

**Purpose**: Build empathy and establish that you understand their world before proposing anything.

### 2. Solution (3–5 slides)
Present what you're offering — the product, service, approach, or decision. Show how it works, what makes it different, and why it's credible. This is where proof points, demos, and differentiators live.

**Purpose**: Show a clear, credible path from pain to relief.

### 3. Benefit (2–3 slides)
Translate features into outcomes the audience cares about. ROI, time saved, risk reduced, competitive advantage gained. This is not what it does — it's what they get.

**Purpose**: Make the value tangible and personal to the decision-makers in the room.

## Slide Skeleton

| Slide | Section | Purpose | Key Question It Answers |
|-------|---------|---------|------------------------|
| 1 | Title | Set expectations | What is this about? |
| 2 | Problem | The pain point | What's hurting right now? |
| 3 | Problem | Impact/cost of the problem | How much is this costing us? |
| 4 | Problem | Why current approaches fail | Why hasn't this been solved? |
| 5 | Solution | What we offer | What's the answer? |
| 6 | Solution | How it works | How does it actually work? |
| 7 | Solution | Differentiators | Why this over alternatives? |
| 8 | Solution | Proof/validation | Who else has this worked for? |
| 9 | Benefit | Quantified outcomes | What do we gain? |
| 10 | Benefit | Timeline to value | How fast do we see results? |
| 11 | Ask | Next steps / CTA | What do you want us to do? |
| 12 | Q&A | Discussion | Open floor |

## Example Transitions

- Problem → Solution: "There's a better way. Let me show you what we've built..."
- Solution → Benefit: "Here's what this means for your bottom line..."
- Benefit → Ask: "To capture these benefits, here's what we need to get started..."
- Problem deepening: "And the cost of doing nothing isn't just financial..."

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Starting with your solution**: Always lead with their problem. If you open with "Our product does X", you've lost the audience before they care.
- **Features instead of benefits**: "Our platform has 99.9% uptime" is a feature. "You'll never lose a deal because the system went down" is a benefit. Always translate.
- **Vague problem statements**: "Companies struggle with efficiency" is too generic. "Your team spends 12 hours per week on manual data reconciliation" hits home.
- **No social proof**: The Solution section needs validation — case studies, testimonials, data, or credible third-party endorsements. Claims without proof are just opinions.
- **Forgetting the ask**: Every pitch must end with a clear, specific call to action. Not "let's stay in touch" but "we'd like to schedule a technical deep-dive next Thursday."
