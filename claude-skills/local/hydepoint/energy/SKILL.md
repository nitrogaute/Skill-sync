# Energy Domain Skill

This skill provides domain-specific knowledge for building business cases in the energy sector, covering renewable energy, conventional power, and emerging clean technologies.

## Overview

The energy sector is undergoing a fundamental transformation driven by climate imperatives, technological innovation, and evolving regulatory frameworks. Business cases in this domain must navigate complex stakeholder ecosystems, long investment horizons, and rapidly shifting market dynamics.

This skill enables structured analysis of energy sector opportunities across:
- Renewable energy generation (wind, solar, hydro)
- Energy storage and grid infrastructure
- Hydrogen economy and alternative fuels
- Conventional energy transition strategies
- Energy efficiency and demand-side management

## Energy Sectors

### Offshore Wind
Offshore wind represents one of the fastest-growing energy sectors globally. Key considerations include:
- Fixed-bottom vs. floating foundation technologies
- Grid connection infrastructure and export cables
- Operation and maintenance (O&M) vessel requirements
- Decommissioning obligations and cost provisioning
- Supply chain localization requirements

### Onshore Wind
Mature technology with established supply chains:
- Turbine size optimization for site conditions
- Community acceptance and benefit-sharing models
- Repowering opportunities at aging wind farms
- Hybrid configurations with storage

### Solar Energy
Solar photovoltaic and concentrated solar power opportunities:
- Utility-scale ground-mounted installations
- Commercial and industrial rooftop systems
- Distributed generation and community solar
- Agrivoltaics and dual land-use models
- Solar thermal for industrial heat applications

### Hydrogen
Emerging hydrogen economy spanning production, storage, and end-use:
- Green hydrogen from electrolysis (renewable-powered)
- Blue hydrogen with carbon capture
- Hydrogen transportation and distribution infrastructure
- Industrial decarbonization applications
- Hydrogen mobility (heavy transport, maritime, aviation)

### Energy Storage
Battery and alternative storage technologies:
- Lithium-ion battery energy storage systems (BESS)
- Long-duration storage (flow batteries, compressed air, gravity)
- Pumped hydro storage
- Grid services and ancillary markets

## Technology Readiness Level (TRL) Guidance

Understanding Technology Readiness Level is critical for energy sector business cases, as it directly impacts risk profiles, financing structures, and go-to-market timelines.

### TRL Scale Overview
- **TRL 1-3**: Basic research and proof of concept
- **TRL 4-5**: Technology validation in laboratory/relevant environment
- **TRL 6-7**: Technology demonstration in relevant/operational environment
- **TRL 8-9**: System complete and qualified/proven in operational environment

### TRL Implications for Business Cases

**TRL 1-3 Technologies:**
- Require grant funding or strategic R&D investment
- Long time horizons (7-15 years to commercialization)
- High technical risk, uncertain market timing
- Value proposition focused on IP development and strategic positioning

**TRL 4-6 Technologies:**
- Suitable for pilot projects and demonstration funding
- Partner with established players for de-risking
- Focus on performance validation and cost reduction pathways
- Build reference cases and performance data

**TRL 7-9 Technologies:**
- Ready for commercial deployment
- Project finance structures available
- LCOE and bankability become primary metrics
- Focus on scaling, supply chain, and market capture

## Market Dynamics

### Energy Transition Trends
The global energy transition is reshaping market fundamentals:
- **Decarbonization mandates**: Net zero commitments driving investment
- **Electrification**: Transport, heating, and industrial processes
- **Renewable cost competitiveness**: Solar and wind now cheapest sources in most markets
- **Grid modernization**: Smart grids, distributed energy, flexibility services
- **Clean energy investment surge**: Trillions in annual capital deployment

### Market Size Considerations
- Total Addressable Market (TAM): Global energy investment ($2T+ annually)
- Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM): Specific technology/geography focus
- Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM): Realistic capture based on competitive position

### Competitive Dynamics
- Incumbent utilities and energy majors pivoting to renewables
- Pure-play renewable developers and IPPs
- Technology providers and OEMs
- Financial investors (infrastructure funds, pension funds)
- New entrants from adjacent sectors (tech, automotive)

## Value Proposition

### Primary Value Drivers

**LCOE (Levelized Cost of Energy)**
The most critical metric for generation assets:
- Capital cost per MW installed
- Capacity factor and energy yield
- Operating costs over project lifetime
- Financing costs and discount rate
- Benchmark against alternatives and grid prices

**Efficiency Improvements**
- Conversion efficiency gains
- Capacity factor optimization
- Loss reduction (electrical, thermal)
- Performance ratio improvements

**Reliability and Availability**
- Uptime and availability guarantees
- Predictive maintenance capabilities
- Weather resilience and climate adaptation
- Grid stability contributions

**Sustainability Credentials**
- Lifecycle carbon footprint
- Circular economy considerations
- Biodiversity impact management
- Social license to operate

**ROI and Financial Returns**
- Project IRR and equity returns
- Payback period
- Risk-adjusted returns
- ESG investment criteria alignment

### Value Proposition Frameworks
- Cost reduction vs. incumbent solutions
- Revenue enhancement opportunities
- Risk mitigation and resilience
- Strategic and regulatory compliance value
- Innovation and learning curve benefits

## Pricing Guidance

### Common Pricing Models

**Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs)**
- Fixed-price long-term contracts (10-25 years)
- Indexed or escalating price structures
- Corporate vs. utility PPA considerations
- Virtual/synthetic PPA structures

**LCOE-Based Pricing**
- Full lifecycle cost recovery
- Technology-specific benchmarks
- Regional cost variations
- Learning curve projections

**Capital Expenditure (Capex) Pricing**
- Per MW or per MWh installed cost
- EPC contract structures
- Equipment supply agreements
- Development fee models

**Operating Expenditure (Opex) Pricing**
- Per MWh O&M costs
- Fixed vs. variable cost components
- Performance-based contracts
- Availability guarantees

**Energy Unit Pricing**
- Price per kWh or MWh
- Time-of-use differentiation
- Merchant price exposure
- Hedging and risk management

### Pricing Considerations
- Technology maturity and risk premiums
- Project scale economies
- Geographic cost variations
- Competitive auction dynamics
- Policy support levels

## Key Metrics

### Project-Level Metrics
- **Capacity Factor**: Actual output vs. theoretical maximum
- **LCOE**: Levelized cost per MWh
- **Capex per MW**: Total installed cost
- **IRR**: Internal rate of return
- **NPV**: Net present value
- **Payback Period**: Years to recover investment

### Operational Metrics
- **Availability**: Operational uptime percentage
- **Performance Ratio**: Actual vs. expected output
- **Curtailment Rate**: Energy lost to grid constraints
- **O&M Cost per MWh**: Operational efficiency

### Market Metrics
- **Pipeline Value**: Development stage project value
- **Contracted Revenue**: Secured offtake agreements
- **Merchant Exposure**: Uncontracted capacity risk
- **Capacity Under Management**: Total operational portfolio

### Financial Metrics
- **Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)**
- **Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)**
- **Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)**
- **Cash Yield**: Annual cash distribution percentage

### ESG and Impact Metrics
- **Carbon Avoidance**: tCO2 avoided per year
- **Homes Powered Equivalent**: Consumer-friendly impact metric
- **Local Job Creation**: Economic impact
- **Community Benefit Funds**: Social value contribution
