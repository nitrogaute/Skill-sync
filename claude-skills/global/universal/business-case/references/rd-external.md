## Collaboration Structures

Choose the structure that matches the commitment level and strategic intent.

| Structure | Commitment | IP Sharing | Governance | Best for |
|-----------|-----------|------------|------------|----------|
| **Research consortium** | Low | Shared/open | Steering committee | Pre-competitive research, standards work |
| **Co-funded R&D** | Medium | Defined per agreement | Joint project board | Shared cost, defined deliverables |
| **Co-development agreement** | Medium-High | Joint ownership or licensing | Joint development team | Building a specific product together |
| **Joint venture (JV)** | High | JV entity owns | JV board of directors | Long-term shared business |
| **Strategic alliance** | Variable | Case-by-case | Relationship management | Multiple collaboration areas |

### Adapting the Five Case Model

| Five Case Section | External R&D Emphasis |
|-------------------|----------------------|
| Strategic Case | Why collaborate? Why this partner? What can't we do alone? |
| Economic Case | Cost-sharing model, value split, alternatives (do alone, different partner, don't do) |
| Commercial Case | IP ownership, licensing, route to market, exclusivity |
| Financial Case | Contribution structure, cost allocation, revenue/benefit sharing |
| Management Case | Joint governance, conflict resolution, cultural integration, exit clauses |

## Partner Evaluation

Assess potential partners across these dimensions before committing.

### Complementarity Assessment

| Dimension | We Bring | Partner Brings | Gap/Overlap |
|-----------|---------|----------------|-------------|
| Technology/IP | | | |
| Market access | | | |
| Manufacturing capability | | | |
| Domain expertise | | | |
| Funding | | | |
| Talent/team | | | |
| Regulatory knowledge | | | |

**The ideal partner fills gaps without creating dependency.** If the partner brings something you could eventually build yourself, the collaboration has a natural expiration date -- factor that into the business case.

### Partner Due Diligence

- **Financial health**: Can they sustain their commitment? (annual report, credit rating)
- **Track record**: Have they delivered on similar collaborations?
- **Cultural fit**: Decision-making speed, risk appetite, communication style
- **Strategic alignment**: Do their long-term goals align or will they diverge?
- **IP portfolio**: Do they have relevant IP? Any IP conflicts?
- **References**: Talk to other organizations they've collaborated with

### Red Flags
- Partner wants access to your IP but is vague about their contribution
- Misaligned timelines (they need results in 6 months, your technology needs 2 years)
- Significant power asymmetry (one partner has all the leverage)
- Overlapping market ambitions (today's partner becomes tomorrow's competitor)
- Reluctance to define IP ownership upfront

## IP and Knowledge Sharing

IP is the most contentious aspect of external R&D. Define it early and clearly.

### IP Ownership Models

| Model | When to Use | Pros | Cons |
|-------|------------|------|------|
| **Joint ownership** | Equal contribution, shared exploitation | Fair, incentivizes both | Complex licensing, deadlock risk |
| **Lead party owns, other licenses** | Unequal contribution | Clear ownership | Licensee has less control |
| **Field-of-use split** | Non-overlapping markets | Both can exploit in their domain | Boundary disputes |
| **JV entity owns** | Joint venture structure | Clean separation | Both lose control to JV |
| **Background/foreground split** | Preserving existing IP | Protects pre-existing assets | Complex tracking |

### Key Principles
- **Background IP** (what each party brings): stays with the originator. Always.
- **Foreground IP** (what's created together): defined by agreement before starting
- **Sideground IP** (developed independently during the collaboration): often contentious, must be addressed
- **Publication rights**: Who can publish what, when? Especially important in academic partnerships.
- **Licensing terms**: If one party owns, what license does the other get? Exclusive? Field-limited? Time-limited?

## Financial Structures

### Cost Sharing Models

| Model | Description | Best for |
|-------|------------|----------|
| **50/50 split** | Equal contribution | Equal partners, equal benefit |
| **Proportional to benefit** | Contribution proportional to expected value capture | Unequal market positions |
| **In-kind + cash** | One contributes expertise, other contributes funding | Large company + startup/university |
| **Milestone-based** | Funding released at milestones | High uncertainty, staged commitment |
| **Revenue-sharing** | Low upfront, share future revenue | Cash-constrained partners |

### Financial Due Diligence for the Business Case

Include in the Financial Case:
1. Total project cost and each party's share
2. Payment schedule and milestone triggers
3. Currency risk (if cross-border)
4. What happens if costs overrun? (pro-rata increase? capped contribution?)
5. Termination costs at each stage
6. Value of the collaboration vs. doing it alone (premium/discount analysis)

## Governance Models

### Joint Steering Committee (most common)
- Representatives from both parties (typically 2-3 from each)
- Meets quarterly (monthly during active development)
- Approves scope changes, budget adjustments, milestone acceptance
- Escalation path for disagreements

### Decision Matrix

| Decision Type | Authority |
|--------------|-----------|
| Technical direction (within scope) | Joint project team |
| Scope changes | Steering committee (unanimous) |
| Budget adjustments (<10%) | Project leads |
| Budget adjustments (>10%) | Steering committee |
| IP licensing to third parties | Both parties must agree |
| Termination | Per agreement terms |
| Dispute resolution | Escalation: project leads -> steering committee -> mediation -> arbitration |

### Communication Structure
- Weekly technical syncs (working level)
- Monthly progress reports (management level)
- Quarterly steering committee reviews
- Ad-hoc escalation protocol for blockers

## Risk Allocation

External R&D adds collaboration-specific risks on top of standard project risks.

### Collaboration-Specific Risks

| Risk | Description | Mitigation |
|------|------------|------------|
| **Partner withdrawal** | Partner loses interest or funding | Milestone-based commitment, exit clauses |
| **IP leakage** | Confidential information shared beyond agreement | NDA, information barriers, audit rights |
| **Cultural clash** | Different work styles, decision speeds | Early alignment workshops, clear processes |
| **Free-riding** | One partner contributes less than agreed | Clear deliverable tracking, milestone reviews |
| **Strategic divergence** | Partner's strategy shifts away from project goals | Annual strategic alignment review, exit options |
| **Talent poaching** | Partner hires your key people | Non-solicitation clauses, team rotation |
| **Competition emergence** | Partner becomes competitor in adjacent area | Field-of-use restrictions, non-compete terms |

### Risk Allocation Principle
Assign each risk to the party best positioned to manage it. Don't concentrate all risk on one side -- it breeds resentment and gaming behavior.

## Exit and Dissolution

Every collaboration agreement must define how it ends. The business case should address exit scenarios.

### Planned Exit (success)
- Project completed, deliverables transferred
- IP ownership finalized per agreement
- Revenue/benefit sharing commences per terms
- Transition period for knowledge transfer

### Early Termination
- **Mutual agreement**: Both parties agree to end. Easiest.
- **Material breach**: One party fails obligations. Cure period, then termination rights.
- **Convenience termination**: Either party can exit with notice period (90-180 days typical)
- **Change of control**: If one party is acquired, the other gets termination option

### Post-Termination
- Background IP returns to originator
- Foreground IP handled per agreement (often: both parties retain non-exclusive license)
- Confidentiality obligations survive (typically 3-5 years)
- Non-compete/non-solicitation periods
- Final accounting and settlement of costs

### Business Case Implication
Include in the Management Case: "If this collaboration ends at Stage [X], our sunk cost is [Y], we retain [Z] IP/knowledge, and we can continue alone / pivot to alternative partner / abandon."
