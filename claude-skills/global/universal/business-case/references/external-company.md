## Market Sizing

### TAM/SAM/SOM Analysis

**TAM (Total Addressable Market):** Total revenue opportunity if 100% market share.

Two approaches:
- **Top-down**: Start with industry reports, narrow by segments. Quick but often inflated.
  - Example: "Global maritime autonomy market is $5.2B (2025). Sensor segment is 15% = $780M."
- **Bottom-up** (preferred): Count potential customers x average deal size.
  - Example: "3,200 vessels in target segment x $150K average system price = $480M."

**SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market):** The portion you can realistically serve given geography, channels, product fit.
- Example: "We serve European maritime. 40% of vessels are in our region = $192M SAM."

**SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):** What you can realistically capture in 2-3 years.
- Example: "5% market share in 3 years = $9.6M annual revenue."

### Market Dynamics
- **Growth rate**: Is this market growing, stable, or declining? Source the data.
- **Cyclicality**: Is demand tied to economic cycles, commodity prices, regulatory changes?
- **Disruption risk**: Are new technologies or business models threatening the market structure?
- **Regulatory trajectory**: Tightening regulation (opportunity for compliance products) or loosening (removing barriers)?

## Due Diligence Framework

### Financial Due Diligence
- Revenue quality: Recurring vs. one-time? Customer concentration?
- Profitability: Gross margin, EBITDA margin, trends
- Working capital: Cash conversion cycle, inventory levels
- Debt and obligations: Loans, leases, contingent liabilities
- Financial projections: Are management's forecasts credible?
- Accounting quality: Revenue recognition policies, any restatements?

### Technology Due Diligence
- Architecture: Modern or legacy? Technical debt level?
- IP portfolio: Patents, trade secrets, open-source dependencies
- Scalability: Can the technology handle 10x growth?
- Security: Vulnerabilities, compliance with standards
- Team: Key developers, bus factor, documentation quality
- Roadmap: Is the technology direction aligned with market trends?

### Commercial Due Diligence
- Customer interviews: Are customers satisfied? Would they renew/expand?
- Pipeline: Current sales pipeline, conversion rates
- Market position: Are they gaining or losing share?
- Pricing power: Can they raise prices without losing customers?
- Channel strategy: Direct, partner, OEM? Scalable?

### Legal Due Diligence
- Contracts: Key customer/supplier contracts, any onerous terms?
- IP: Ownership clear? Any disputes or claims?
- Regulatory: Compliance status, pending regulatory changes?
- Litigation: Any current or threatened legal actions?
- Employment: Key contracts, non-competes, equity commitments

### HR/Cultural Due Diligence
- Key person risk: Who are the critical people? Flight risk?
- Compensation structure: Aligned with our model?
- Culture: Decision-making style, innovation culture, work ethic
- Integration: Expected attrition post-acquisition

## Valuation Approaches

### Revenue Multiples
- **EV/Revenue**: Enterprise value / annual revenue. Common for high-growth companies.
- Typical ranges by sector (use as sanity check, not valuation):
  - SaaS: 5-15x (higher for high-growth, net retention >120%)
  - Hardware/industrial: 1-3x
  - Services: 1-2x
  - Deep tech/early stage: 10-30x (speculative)

### Earnings Multiples
- **EV/EBITDA**: Enterprise value / EBITDA. Standard for profitable companies.
- Typical ranges: 8-15x for industrial tech, 15-25x for high-growth software

### DCF (Discounted Cash Flow)
- Project future free cash flows for 5-10 years
- Apply discount rate (WACC or hurdle rate)
- Calculate terminal value (perpetuity growth or exit multiple)
- Sum discounted cash flows + terminal value
- Sensitivity: run at multiple discount rates and growth assumptions

### Comparable Transactions
- Find recent acquisitions of similar companies
- Calculate implied multiples
- Adjust for size, growth, profitability differences
- Most credible valuation method for M&A business cases

### For the Business Case
Present 2-3 valuation approaches and the range they produce. A single point estimate is misleading. Show:
- Low end (conservative assumptions)
- Base case (management/analyst consensus)
- High end (optimistic assumptions)
- Compare to the asking price or expected deal value

## Integration Considerations

Include in the Management Case if this is an acquisition or deep partnership.

### Integration Planning
- **Day 1 readiness**: What must work on day one? (payroll, IT systems, customer support)
- **Quick wins (30-90 days)**: Cross-selling, eliminating duplicate costs, talent retention bonuses
- **Medium-term (3-12 months)**: Product integration, org restructuring, system migration
- **Long-term (1-3 years)**: Full technology integration, brand consolidation, culture alignment

### Integration Risks

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Key talent departure | High | High | Retention packages, early communication |
| Customer churn | Medium | High | Joint customer outreach, service continuity |
| Technology integration delays | Medium | Medium | Phased approach, dedicated integration team |
| Culture clash | Medium | Medium | Cultural assessment pre-close, integration workshops |
| Hidden liabilities | Low | High | Thorough due diligence, representations & warranties |

### Synergy Realization
Synergies are the most over-promised and under-delivered aspect of acquisitions. Be specific:
- **Revenue synergies**: Name the specific cross-sell/up-sell opportunities. Discount by 50% for the business case.
- **Cost synergies**: Name the specific duplicate roles, systems, facilities. These are more reliable but take longer than expected.
- **Dis-synergies**: What will cost MORE after acquisition? (integration costs, retention packages, system migration, management distraction)

Rule of thumb: Integration costs typically equal 5-10% of deal value. If your business case doesn't include integration costs, it's incomplete.
