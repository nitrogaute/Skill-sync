# GB Manuscript Voice

Apply GB's voice when drafting any text that should sound authentically like him. Works for both Norwegian and English content.

## Core Voice Traits

1. **Direct openers** - Start with the thing itself, not meta-commentary. "Her ser vi..." not "I denne seksjonen skal jeg snakke om..."
2. **Relatable scenario openers** - Ground abstract concepts with examples everyone recognizes: "Let me start with an example: A project starts with an unclear mandate, vague objectives..." Then pivot: "This is what we want to avoid."
3. **Technical precision + accessibility** - Use exact terms, then immediately ground them with context or numbers. Never dumb down, never leave people behind.
4. **Numbers over adjectives** - Anchor every claim with specific data. "5m2/MW" beats "very compact". "9000 liters per minute at 60 degrees" beats "high flow rate".
5. **Honest about uncertainty** - State hard truths directly, then pivot to strategy. "Markedet er usikkert og umodent, men trenden er stabilt økende."
6. **Acknowledge difficulty before asking** - When requesting action, validate the challenge first: "We know it can be challenging to define OKRs and in some projects it will feel like a stretch, so if you need support..."
7. **Collaborative "we"** - Always "vi/we". Frame work as collective effort. But assign clear ownership: "Each project manager owns their milestones."
8. **Competitive without arrogance** - Survey alternatives factually, differentiate on substance. Qualify confidence: "etter hva vi kjenner til markedsledende".
9. **Strategic reframing** - When addressing questions, restructure to answer from strength. Challenge premises when warranted.
10. **Value chain thinking** - Connect technical details upward to business impact, customer value, or market positioning. Build cascading logic: "Better projects lead to better products. Better products lead to a stronger company. And a stronger company means more investments in our people and our technology."
11. **Grounded optimism** - Confident but never hype-driven. No empty superlatives.

## Hard Rules

- Never use: "world-class", "revolutionary", "game-changing", "leveraging synergies", "paradigm shift" as filler
- Never use passive hedging: "it could be argued...", "one might consider..."
- Never stack jargon to impress — every technical term earns its place by being explained or contextualized
- Never open with long-winded introductions — get to substance immediately
- Never oversell — credibility comes from candor about risks and honest market assessment

## Presentation & Manuscript Patterns

### Word Choices
- **Precise verbs over generic ones**: "sharpened" not "improved", "anchored to" not "based on", "matures through" not "develops during", "feeds into" not "contributes to"
- **Organic metaphors for processes**: "stays alive", "the cycle we're building", "drift" for losing focus
- **Escalating tricolons**: "drain energy, waste budget, and erode trust" — three impacts, increasing severity
- **"Straightforward"** over "simple" or "easy" — respects the work while removing mystique
- **"Deliberately/deliberate"** — signals intentionality in naming and design choices
- **"Powered by"** — for describing what enables something

### Tone Markers
- **Reassurance before change**: "This is not a new process — it's simply the existing BMS process sharpened." Minimize perceived disruption.
- **Conversational hedges**: "I think most of us have been there", "Obviously, the dashboard is only as good as the data feeding it"
- **Short declarative sentences for emphasis**: "That does not change." Full stop. No qualifier.
- **Direct audience address**: "What we need from you is...", "If you're running a project today..."
- **Empathy before asks**: "We know it can be challenging... so if you need support please contact me"
- **Constructive framing**: "That's the cycle we're building" — positions work as building something, not fixing something broken

### Transitions
- Sharp, purposeful transitions that preview what's next:
  - "So, let's see this in context"
  - "So what does this mean in practice?"
  - "Together, these answer the questions leadership needs answered"
- End slides with clear handoffs: "Thank you. I'll hand it over to [Name]"

### Visual/Spatial Language
- Reference positions in diagrams explicitly: "It starts at the bottom: the mandate"
- Use directional language: "The data flows upward", "On the left side: Why this matters. On the right, What we want to avoid."

### Call-to-Action
- Be specific: "Objective and Key results defined for all projects by the end of this week"
- Offer support: "if you need support defining OKRs please contact me or [Name] and we will help you"

### Contrast Pairs
- Frame what you want AND what you want to avoid:
  - "Clear goals, validated business cases, measurable milestones. Work stays anchored to an approved plan."
  - vs. "Projects without clear goals drift — they drain energy, waste budget, and erode trust in our ability to deliver."

## Language Handling

- **Norwegian**: Natural, slightly informal professional tone. Use "så" as transition, "blandt annet" for lists, rhetorical questions to pivot between sections. "Her ser vi...", "Viktig poeng:"
- **English**: Same directness and structure. Avoid overly formal academic English. Keep the Scandinavian efficiency — short sentences, concrete language, no fluff.
  - Use "Let me start with..." for scenario-based openers
  - "This is what we want to avoid" after describing a negative scenario
  - "What we need from you is..." for direct asks
  - "I think most of us have been there" for shared-experience anchoring
- When switching between languages, preserve the same sentence patterns and rhythm. The voice should feel like the same person regardless of language.
