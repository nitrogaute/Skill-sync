# Strategic Narrative Framework

A structured approach to crafting compelling change narratives that drive organizational transformation.
Based on Andy Raskin's strategic narrative methodology, enriched with change management research from
Kotter, Heath brothers (Made to Stick), and identity-based persuasion from James March.

## When to Use This Skill

This framework is designed for situations where you need to move people — not just inform them.
It's particularly effective when:

- Introducing new processes, tools, or governance models
- Driving behavioral change across teams or departments
- Building executive buy-in for transformation initiatives
- Communicating "why" behind operational improvements
- Creating internal presentations, memos, or town-hall scripts
- Framing technology adoption or digital transformation

It is NOT the right tool for product marketing to external customers, investor pitch decks (use
a pitch-deck skill instead), or purely informational updates with no call to action.

## The Five-Act Structure

Every strategic change narrative follows this arc. The sequence matters — skipping or reordering
steps weakens the narrative significantly.

### Act 1: Name the Undeniable Shift

Open by describing an external change that your audience already senses but may not have
articulated. This is NOT about your organization's problems — it's about the world changing
around you.

**Why it works:** Starting with an external shift removes defensiveness. Nobody is being blamed.
The audience naturally asks "what does this mean for us?" which is exactly the mindset you want.

**Characteristics of a strong shift:**
- Observable and hard to argue with
- External to the organization (market, technology, regulation, competition)
- Already felt by the audience even if unnamed
- Creates a fork in the road: adapt or fall behind

**Common patterns by domain:**

| Domain | Example Shifts |
|--------|---------------|
| R&D / Innovation | Funding cycles shortening; competitors making faster portfolio decisions; regulatory timelines tightening |
| Technology adoption | Industry moving to real-time data; manual processes becoming competitive liabilities |
| Operational excellence | Board/investor expectations for transparency increasing; talent expecting modern tooling |
| Energy / Maritime | Decarbonization timelines accelerating; capital discipline intensifying |

**Template:**
> "Something fundamental has changed in [our industry / the way X works].
> [Describe the shift in 1-2 concrete sentences with evidence].
> Organizations that recognize this shift early will [positive outcome].
> Those that don't will [negative consequence]."

**Example:**
> "The era of R&D funded on intuition is ending. Our investors, our board, and our partners
> now expect the same data rigor from research portfolios that they've long demanded from
> manufacturing and operations. The organizations winning the next cycle aren't necessarily
> spending more — they're deciding faster because they can see what's working."

### Act 2: Show the Stakes (Winners and Losers)

Paint a vivid picture of two futures: organizations that adapt vs. those that don't.
This creates emotional urgency without resorting to fear-mongering.

**Key principles:**
- Show, don't tell. Use specific examples or composite scenarios.
- Name real competitors or peers if appropriate — "Company X reduced their go/no-go cycle from 9 months to 6 weeks"
- Make the "loser" scenario recognizable — describe situations your audience has personally experienced
- Keep it balanced: ~60% on the positive future, ~40% on the risk of inaction

**Pain point anchoring technique:**
Reference specific, lived frustrations your audience will recognize. These micro-stories
create instant emotional resonance:

- "The board meeting where nobody could answer how many active projects we have"
- "The quarterly review where we discovered a project was 6 months behind — after the fact"
- "The resource allocation discussion based entirely on who argued loudest"

**Template:**
> "We've all been in [recognizable frustrating scenario]. That's not a people problem —
> it's a visibility problem. Meanwhile, [peer/competitor] has moved to [better approach]
> and is seeing [concrete result]."

### Act 3: Paint the Promised Land

Describe the desired end-state as a vivid, aspirational picture. This is NOT a feature list
or a process description — it's what life looks like when the transformation succeeds.

**Critical rule:** The Promised Land is about outcomes and capabilities, never about tools or
processes. "We use Dashboard X" is not a Promised Land. "Any leader can answer 'how is our
portfolio performing?' in under 60 seconds" IS a Promised Land.

**Characteristics of a compelling Promised Land:**
- Described in present tense ("We are an organization where...")
- Focuses on what people CAN DO, not what they MUST DO
- Specific enough to be measurable but aspirational enough to inspire
- Connects to organizational identity ("This is who we're becoming")

**Template:**
> "Imagine [time horizon]. [Your organization] is a place where:
> - [Capability statement 1 — what leaders can now do]
> - [Capability statement 2 — what teams experience differently]
> - [Capability statement 3 — what the outside world sees]
> This isn't about [tool/process]. It's about becoming [identity statement]."

**Example:**
> "Twelve months from now, every project in our R&D portfolio has a live health score.
> Leadership doesn't need to schedule a meeting to know where we stand — they can see it.
> Teams get early warnings when timelines or budgets drift, and they have the authority to
> course-correct before small issues become large ones. We're not just better informed —
> we're fundamentally faster at turning research into value."

### Act 4: Show the Bridge

Now — and only now — introduce the concrete changes. The audience has been primed to want
a solution. The bridge answers "how do we get from here to the Promised Land?"

**Structure the bridge as a journey, not a mandate:**

1. **Phase 1: Foundation** (what happens first, typically 0-3 months)
   - Quick wins that demonstrate the new way works
   - Minimum viable process changes
   - Pilot teams or projects

2. **Phase 2: Scale** (building momentum, typically 3-6 months)
   - Expanding to more teams/projects
   - Refining based on pilot learnings
   - Building habits and routines

3. **Phase 3: Embed** (the new normal, typically 6-12 months)
   - Full organizational adoption
   - Continuous improvement cycles
   - Culture shift visible in daily behavior

**For each phase, specify:**
- What changes concretely (new meetings, reports, tools, KPIs)
- Who is responsible
- What success looks like at the end of the phase
- What stays the same (this reduces anxiety)

**Anti-pattern to avoid:** Don't present a 47-item implementation plan. The bridge should
feel achievable. Three phases with 2-3 key actions each is the right level of detail for
a narrative. Save the full plan for appendices or follow-up documents.

### Act 5: Prove It's Possible

Close with evidence that this transformation is achievable. This overcomes the "sounds great
but it'll never work here" objection.

**Types of proof (use 2-3, in order of persuasiveness):**

1. **Internal pilots** — "We tested this with Team X and saw [result]" (strongest)
2. **Peer examples** — "Company Y in our industry did this and achieved [result]"
3. **Expert validation** — Research, frameworks, or advisory endorsements
4. **Quick demonstrations** — Live demos, prototype dashboards, mock reports
5. **Logical inevitability** — "If we do X, Y naturally follows because [mechanism]"

**Template:**
> "This isn't theoretical. [Proof point 1 — ideally internal].
> [Proof point 2 — peer or industry example].
> We're not asking anyone to take a leap of faith — we're asking you to look at the evidence."

The five-act structure adapts to multiple formats. Read `references/delivery-formats.md`
for detailed templates for each of these:

- **Executive presentation** (10-15 slides)
- **Town hall script** (15-20 minute talk)
- **Written memo / one-pager** (for email or async distribution)
- **Workshop facilitation guide** (interactive half-day session)

## Narrative Quality Checklist

Before finalizing any narrative, verify these criteria:

**Structure:**
- [ ] Opens with external shift, NOT internal problems
- [ ] Stakes section includes both positive and negative futures
- [ ] Promised Land describes outcomes, not tools/processes
- [ ] Bridge has clear phases with manageable scope
- [ ] Proof points are concrete and verifiable

**Emotional resonance:**
- [ ] Contains at least 2 specific micro-stories the audience will recognize
- [ ] Uses present tense for the Promised Land (creates immediacy)
- [ ] Frames change as identity ("who we're becoming") not compliance ("what you must do")
- [ ] Balances urgency with optimism (~40/60 split)

**Practical clarity:**
- [ ] A listener can explain the change to a colleague in 2 sentences
- [ ] First concrete action is clear and achievable within 2 weeks
- [ ] "What stays the same" is explicitly addressed (reduces anxiety)
- [ ] Success metrics are defined for at least the first phase

**Audience calibration:**
- [ ] Language matches the audience (executives vs. team leads vs. individual contributors)
- [ ] Technical depth is appropriate
- [ ] Anticipated objections are pre-addressed in the narrative flow

## Identity-Based Persuasion Layer

Research by James March shows that people make decisions based on three questions:
1. Who am I? (identity)
2. What kind of situation is this? (recognition)
3. What would someone like me do? (action)

Weave identity framing throughout the narrative:

- **Act 1:** "Organizations like ours — those at the forefront of [domain] — are recognizing..."
- **Act 3:** "We become the kind of organization that..."
- **Act 4:** "As [identity], we naturally..."
- **Act 5:** "This is already how our best teams operate..."

This is significantly more motivating than rational cost-benefit arguments alone, especially
for senior professionals who see themselves as leaders in their field.

## Common Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Opening with "we have a problem" | Triggers defensiveness | Open with external shift instead |
| Listing features/tools as the solution | Feels like a vendor pitch | Describe outcomes people experience |
| Skipping to the bridge | No emotional buy-in for change | Build the case with Acts 1-3 first |
| Fear-only messaging | Creates paralysis, not action | Balance with aspirational Promised Land |
| Abstract jargon ("leverage synergies") | No emotional resonance | Use concrete micro-stories |
| One narrative for all audiences | Different groups need different emphasis | Adapt the Acts, keep the structure |

## Workflow

When a user asks for help crafting a change narrative:

1. **Understand the context** — Ask about the organization, the change being introduced, the target audience, and what resistance they anticipate.
2. **Identify the external shift** — Help the user find the "undeniable change" relevant to their industry/situation. This is often the hardest part.
3. **Draft the five acts** — Work through each act sequentially. Don't skip ahead.
4. **Apply the quality checklist** — Review the draft against all criteria.
5. **Adapt to delivery format** — Shape the narrative for the specific medium (presentation, memo, talk).
6. **Suggest a micro-story inventory** — Help the user identify 3-5 specific moments/frustrations their audience will recognize, for use as anchoring stories.

If the user already has partial content (e.g., a slide deck about a new process), help them
restructure it into the five-act framework rather than starting from scratch.
