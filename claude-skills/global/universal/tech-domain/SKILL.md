# Technology Domain Skill

This skill provides domain-specific knowledge and guidance for developing business cases in the technology sector, including software, platforms, and digital services.

## Overview

The technology sector encompasses a wide range of business models and market dynamics, from enterprise software to consumer applications, from SaaS platforms to API-first services. This skill helps structure business cases that resonate with tech-savvy stakeholders, investors, and decision-makers who understand the unique economics and growth patterns of technology businesses.

Technology business cases must address rapid market evolution, network effects, platform dynamics, and the critical importance of product-market fit. Unlike traditional industries, technology businesses often prioritize growth over immediate profitability, making metrics like Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) and Lifetime Value (LTV) central to the business narrative.

## Business Models

Technology companies typically operate under one or more of the following business models:

### SaaS (Software as a Service)
- Cloud-hosted software delivered on a subscription basis
- Recurring revenue model with predictable cash flows
- Key focus on retention, expansion, and reducing churn
- Examples: Salesforce, HubSpot, Slack, Notion

### Perpetual License
- One-time purchase with optional maintenance contracts
- Higher upfront revenue but less predictable long-term income
- Often combined with support and update subscriptions
- Traditional model for enterprise software (e.g., legacy ERP systems)

### API-First / Platform
- Revenue from API calls, transactions, or platform usage
- Enables ecosystem development and third-party integrations
- Network effects can create strong competitive moats
- Examples: Stripe, Twilio, AWS

### Marketplace / Transaction-Based
- Takes a percentage of transactions facilitated
- Requires achieving liquidity on both supply and demand sides
- Strong network effects once critical mass is achieved

### Hardware + Software Bundle
- Combined hardware and software offering
- Higher barriers to entry but also higher capital requirements
- Examples: IoT devices, embedded systems

## Market Dynamics

The technology market is characterized by rapid change, winner-take-most dynamics, and the constant emergence of new categories.

### Key Market Forces
- **Digital transformation acceleration**: Enterprises investing heavily in modernization
- **Cloud migration**: Shift from on-premise to cloud-native solutions
- **AI/ML integration**: Growing expectation for intelligent features
- **Security and compliance**: Increasing regulatory requirements
- **Remote/hybrid work**: Permanent shift in how software is consumed

### Market Size Considerations
- Total Addressable Market (TAM): The entire market opportunity
- Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM): The portion you can realistically serve
- Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM): The portion you can capture in the near term

### Competitive Dynamics
- Low barriers to entry but high barriers to scale
- Winner-take-most in platform businesses
- Feature commoditization drives continuous innovation
- Open source as both threat and opportunity

## Technology Adoption Curve

Understanding where your target customers fall on the technology adoption curve is critical for positioning and go-to-market strategy.

### Adoption Segments

1. **Innovators** (2.5% of market)
   - Technology enthusiasts who want to try new things
   - Willing to tolerate rough edges and incomplete products
   - Provide valuable early feedback but limited revenue potential

2. **Early Adopters** (13.5% of market)
   - Visionaries who see strategic advantage in new technology
   - Willing to take risks for competitive advantage
   - Can serve as references and case studies
   - Often have budget authority and influence

3. **Early Majority** (34% of market)
   - Pragmatists who want proven solutions
   - Need references, case studies, and ROI evidence
   - Represent the first major revenue opportunity

4. **Late Majority** (34% of market)
   - Conservatives who adopt when technology becomes standard
   - Price-sensitive and risk-averse
   - Require mature, stable products with full support

5. **Laggards** (16% of market)
   - Skeptics who resist change
   - Only adopt when absolutely necessary
   - Often prefer traditional alternatives

### Crossing the Chasm

The "chasm" between early adopters and the early majority represents a critical transition point. Strategies for crossing include:
- Focus on a specific beachhead segment
- Build a complete "whole product" solution
- Develop compelling references and case studies
- Establish credibility through partnerships and integrations

## Key Metrics

Technology businesses are evaluated on specific metrics that reflect their growth trajectory and unit economics.

### Revenue Metrics
- **MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue)**: Monthly subscription revenue
- **ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue)**: Annualized subscription revenue
- **Revenue Growth Rate**: Year-over-year or month-over-month growth
- **Net Revenue Retention (NRR)**: Revenue from existing customers including expansion and churn

### Customer Economics
- **CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)**: Total cost to acquire a new customer
- **LTV (Lifetime Value)** or **CLV (Customer Lifetime Value)**: Total revenue expected from a customer
- **LTV:CAC Ratio**: Should be >3:1 for healthy SaaS businesses
- **CAC Payback Period**: Months to recover acquisition cost

### Engagement and Retention
- **Churn Rate**: Percentage of customers lost per period (logo churn)
- **Revenue Churn**: Percentage of revenue lost per period
- **Net Promoter Score (NPS)**: Customer satisfaction and loyalty indicator
- **Daily/Monthly Active Users (DAU/MAU)**: Engagement intensity

### Efficiency Metrics
- **Burn Rate**: Monthly cash consumption
- **Runway**: Months of operation remaining at current burn
- **Rule of 40**: Growth rate + profit margin should exceed 40%
- **Magic Number**: Efficiency of sales and marketing spend

## Competitive Landscape

Analyzing the competitive landscape in technology requires understanding different competitive categories.

### Direct Competitors
- Companies offering similar solutions to the same customer segment
- Compete on features, price, user experience, and integrations

### Indirect Competitors
- Alternative approaches to solving the same problem
- May include manual processes, spreadsheets, or adjacent software

### Potential Entrants
- Large platforms that could expand into your space
- Well-funded startups targeting similar opportunities
- Open source projects that could commoditize your offering

### Competitive Positioning Strategies
- Feature differentiation (more capable)
- Price differentiation (more affordable)
- Segment focus (better fit for specific use cases)
- Integration depth (works better with existing tools)
- User experience (easier to use and adopt)

## Value Proposition

Technology value propositions must clearly articulate the benefits in terms that resonate with buyers and users.

### Core Value Drivers

- **Scalability**: Ability to handle growth without proportional cost increase
- **Integration**: Seamless connection with existing systems and workflows
- **Automation**: Reducing manual effort and human error
- **ROI (Return on Investment)**: Quantifiable financial benefit
- **Time to Value**: Speed of deployment and realization of benefits
- **Productivity**: Enabling teams to accomplish more with less effort

### Value Proposition Framework

1. **For** [target customer segment]
2. **Who** [statement of need or opportunity]
3. **Our product is** [product category]
4. **That** [statement of key benefit]
5. **Unlike** [primary competitive alternative]
6. **Our product** [statement of primary differentiation]

### Quantifying Value
- Cost savings (reduced labor, infrastructure, licensing)
- Revenue enablement (new capabilities, faster time to market)
- Risk reduction (compliance, security, reliability)
- Productivity gains (time saved, errors avoided)

## Go-to-Market

Technology go-to-market strategies vary significantly based on average deal size, product complexity, and target customer segment.

### Enterprise Sales
- High-touch, relationship-driven sales process
- Longer sales cycles (3-12+ months)
- Multiple stakeholders and procurement processes
- Requires sales engineers, solution architects, and customer success
- Higher CAC but larger deal sizes and longer retention

### Product-Led Growth (PLG)
- Product serves as the primary driver of acquisition and expansion
- Self-service onboarding and freemium or free trial models
- Focus on user experience and viral/network effects
- Lower CAC but requires excellent product and onboarding
- Examples: Slack, Zoom, Notion, Figma

### Freemium Strategy
- Free tier provides value and drives adoption
- Premium features unlock additional capabilities
- Conversion optimization is critical metric
- Must balance free tier value with upgrade incentive

### Channel and Partnership
- Leverage existing relationships and distribution networks
- Technology partnerships and integrations
- Reseller and system integrator relationships
- Co-selling and marketplace listings

### Marketing Approaches
- Content marketing and thought leadership
- Developer relations and community building
- Event marketing and sponsorships
- Paid acquisition (search, social, display)
- Account-based marketing (ABM) for enterprise

## Customer Segments

Technology products typically target one or more of the following customer segments.

### Enterprise
- Large organizations (typically 1000+ employees)
- Complex requirements and procurement processes
- Need for security, compliance, and support guarantees
- Longer sales cycles but higher deal values
- Often require customization and professional services

### Mid-Market
- Medium-sized organizations (100-1000 employees)
- Growing sophistication in technology adoption
- Balance of self-service and sales-assisted buying
- Sweet spot for many SaaS companies

### SMB (Small and Medium Business)
- Smaller organizations (<100 employees)
- Price-sensitive and value-conscious
- Prefer simple, self-service solutions
- Higher volume, lower deal size
- Higher churn but faster sales cycles

### Startup
- Early-stage companies with specific needs
- Often tech-savvy and willing to try new solutions
- Limited budgets but potential for growth
- Can provide valuable feedback and references

### Developer
- Technical buyers who evaluate based on API quality, documentation, and developer experience
- Community-driven discovery and adoption
- Bottoms-up adoption within organizations
- Require SDKs, documentation, and technical support

## Pricing

Pricing strategy in technology must balance value capture with adoption and competitive positioning.

### Pricing Models

- **Subscription**: Monthly or annual recurring fee
- **Tiered Pricing**: Multiple plans at different price points with increasing features
- **Per Seat / Per User**: Pricing based on number of users
- **Usage-Based**: Pricing based on consumption (API calls, storage, transactions)
- **Freemium**: Free basic tier with paid upgrades
- **Hybrid**: Combination of base subscription plus usage-based components

### Pricing Strategy Considerations
- Value-based pricing (price based on value delivered)
- Competitive pricing (price relative to alternatives)
- Cost-plus pricing (price based on delivery costs)
- Penetration pricing (lower price to gain market share)
- Skimming (higher price for differentiated offering)

### Common Pricing Structures
- Good/Better/Best tier structure
- Annual discount (typically 15-20% for annual commitment)
- Volume discounts for larger deployments
- Enterprise pricing with custom quotes

### Key Pricing Metrics
- Average Revenue Per User (ARPU)
- Average Contract Value (ACV)
- Price elasticity and willingness to pay
- Competitive price benchmarking
