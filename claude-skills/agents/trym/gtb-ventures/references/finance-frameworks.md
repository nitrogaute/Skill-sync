# Finance & CFO Frameworks

## Profitability Metrics
*Source: "How a CFO Evaluates Profitability" — Bill Hannah*

### Gross Margin
- **Formula:** (Revenue - COGS) ÷ Revenue
- Tells you how much you make per unit sold before operating expenses
- Benchmarks vary by industry: food manufacturing ~30%, software 80-90%
- Reveals product-level health: unit pricing, production costs, supplier relationships

### Profit Margin (Net)
- **Formula:** Net Income ÷ Revenue
- Reveals overall operational efficiency (not just product economics)
- Poor profit margin → investigate: selling/marketing costs, G&A, R&D, depreciation, interest
- A company can have great gross margin but terrible profit margin if ops are bloated

### CFO Decision Framework
- Use gross margin for product-level decisions (keep/kill products, supplier negotiation)
- Use profit margin for operational decisions (headcount, refinancing, cost structure)
- Both together = comprehensive financial health in minutes

## Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
*Source: "Why CFOs Obsess Over COGs"*

### Why It Matters
1. **Unit economics** — determines what's left per sale for marketing & R&D
2. **Valuation** — VCs and PE firms use gross margin to assess company worth

### COGS by Business Type
| Component | CPG (e.g. Nike) | SaaS (e.g. Salesforce) |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Materials | Raw materials, packaging | Server costs (AWS/Azure) |
| Labor | Factory floor workers | Customer support, implementation teams |
| Overhead | Factory rent, equipment depreciation, utilities | Third-party API tools |
| Other | Freight-in | — |

### Typical Gross Margins
- **CPG:** 20-40% (high tangible costs)
- **SaaS:** 70-90% (low marginal cost)

### Common COGS Mistakes
- **Manufacturing:** Not tracking waste/theft; misclassifying freight-out as COGS (it's opex)
- **SaaS:** Putting customer success (upsell team) in COGS instead of opex; ignoring escalating server costs; misclassifying revenue-share partnerships as COGS (they're marketing)

## 3-Statement Financial Model
*Source: "Build a 3-Statement Financial Model"*

### The Three Statements
1. **P&L (Income Statement):** Revenue → COGS → Gross Profit → OpEx → Net Income
2. **Balance Sheet:** Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity (most important statement)
3. **Cash Flow Statement:** Converts accrual accounting to actual cash movements

### Critical Connections
- P&L depreciation → accumulates into balance sheet accumulated depreciation
- P&L amortization → accumulates into balance sheet accumulated amortization  
- Net income → accumulates into retained earnings
- Cash flow statement = derived from P&L + balance sheet changes

### Cash Flow Statement (Indirect Method)
- **Operating:** Net income + non-cash expenses (depreciation) ± working capital changes
- **Investing:** Cash spent/received on fixed & intangible assets
- **Financing:** Cash from debt, equity, or distributions
- **Key rule:** ↑ current assets = ↓ cash flow; ↑ current liabilities = ↑ cash flow
- Cash is the "plug" that balances everything

### Model Best Practices
- Include actuals alongside projections
- Color coding: blue = editable assumptions, purple = linked cells, black = calculations, red = error checks
- Use driver tabs for assumptions
- Summary tabs for stakeholder presentation

## Buffett's Balance Sheet Rules
*Source: "Buffett: Analyze a BALANCE SHEET"*

### 5 Quick Rules to Evaluate Any Company
1. **Cash > Debt** — Compare cash & equivalents to total debt. Strong companies don't need debt.
2. **Debt-to-Equity < 0.8** — Total liabilities ÷ shareholders' equity. Finance primarily through equity.
3. **Zero preferred stock** — Presence of preferred stock = company needed hybrid funding (weakness signal)
4. **Retained earnings growing** — Especially through recessions. Shows ability to generate & reinvest profits.
5. **Positive treasury stock** — Indicates buybacks = management returning capital to shareholders.

### Application Example (Chipotle)
- ✅ $1.42B cash, zero debt
- ✅ No preferred stock
- ✅ Consistently growing retained earnings
- ✅ $5B+ treasury stock from buybacks
